üîÅ 1. Excess Inventory

Excess inventory refers to the quantity of products that remain unsold due to demand being lower than forecasted. In our simulation, we observed that the No Postponement strategy resulted in significantly higher excess inventory. This is because each product is stocked independently with its own safety buffer, and variability in demand means some products end up overstocked. On the other hand, the Postponement strategy pools inventory across all products, effectively reducing variability and the need for high individual buffers. As a result, less inventory remains unused. This behavior highlights one of the key advantages of postponement: minimizing holding costs and reducing capital locked in inventory that doesn't generate value.

‚ùå 2. Stockout

Stockout represents the amount of demand that could not be fulfilled due to insufficient inventory. In our simulation, No Postponement led to more frequent and larger stockouts. This is because inventory is pre-allocated to each product based on forecasts, and when actual demand exceeds forecast for any product, the system cannot reallocate excess inventory from other products. In contrast, Postponement uses a shared inventory pool that can flexibly fulfill whichever product sees higher demand, significantly reducing the occurrence of stockouts. This demonstrates the improved responsiveness of the postponement approach in uncertain demand scenarios.

üìà 3. Service Level

Service level is a measure of a supply chain's ability to meet customer demand, calculated as the ratio of fulfilled demand to actual demand. The simulation results showed that Postponement consistently delivered higher service levels compared to No Postponement. This is because pooled inventory allows for dynamic allocation across products, which maintains service even when demand is volatile. In contrast, No Postponement suffers from rigid allocations, and any mismatch between forecast and reality for a specific product negatively impacts service. This makes postponement a more resilient strategy when maintaining customer satisfaction is critical.

üí∞ 4. Total Cost

Total cost in the simulation includes both holding costs (from excess inventory) and shortage costs (from stockouts). Because No Postponement strategy tends to overstock some products while understocking others, it incurs higher costs on both fronts. Postponement, through risk pooling, reduces the need for large safety stocks and ensures better allocation of resources, which leads to lower average costs across the simulation runs. The cost savings observed demonstrate that resilience, when implemented through strategies like postponement, not only supports service levels but can also be cost-effective.


Simulation-method and Calculations: Postponement vs. No Postponement

The step-by-step process, methodology, and calculations used in a simulation to compare two inventory strategies under demand uncertainty: one with product postponement and one without. The goal is to assess which strategy better supports supply chain resilience.

Step 1: Define Product Forecasts and Uncertainty

Three distinct products (A, B, C) are considered. Each product has a unique forecasted mean demand and a different standard deviation representing demand variability. This setup reflects real-world unpredictability in customer demand across product categories.

Step 2: Simulate Actual Demand

For each iteration in the simulation, actual demand is randomly generated for each product using a normal distribution based on its respective forecast and standard deviation. This simulates real-life uncertainty where demand fluctuates around expected values.

Step 3: Determine Inventory Levels

Case 1: No Postponement

Each product is produced and stocked individually. Safety stock is calculated using the formula:

Inventory = Forecast + Z * Standard Deviation

Where Z represents the service level factor (e.g., 1.65 for 95% confidence).

Case 2: With Postponement

A single pooled inventory of base products is held. Safety stock is calculated using risk pooling:

  • Total Forecast = Sum of product forecasts
  • Pooled Standard Deviation = Square root of the sum of variances
  • Pooled Inventory = Total Forecast + Z * Pooled Standard Deviation

This method reduces overall inventory by consolidating variability.

Step 4: Fulfillment and Shortage Calculations

No Postponement

Each product's actual demand is compared to its inventory. Fulfilled demand is the lesser of the two. Excess inventory and stockouts are calculated accordingly.

With Postponement

Total actual demand across all products is compared to the pooled inventory. Fulfilled demand is the minimum of total demand and pooled inventory. Excess and stockouts are derived from the difference.

Step 5: Cost Calculation

Two types of costs are calculated:

  • Holding Cost = Cost per unit of excess inventory
  • Shortage Cost = Cost per unit of unmet demand

Total Cost = (Holding Cost * Excess Inventory) + (Shortage Cost * Stockout)

Step 6: Service Level Calculation

Service Level = Fulfilled Demand / Total Actual Demand

This reflects the proportion of demand that is successfully met.

Step 7: Repeat Simulation

The simulation is run for 1,000 iterations. In each iteration, actual demand, fulfillment, costs, and service levels are recalculated. Results are stored for comparison.

Step 8: Analyze Results

The average performance across all simulations is computed for each strategy. Key performance indicators compared include:

  • Stockouts
  • Excess inventory
  • Service level
  • Total cost

Summary of Findings:

The simulation demonstrates that the postponement strategy:

  • Requires less total inventory (thanks to risk pooling)
  • Achieves higher service levels under variability
  • Reduces both excess and stockouts
  • Lowers overall supply chain cost

This confirms that postponement is an effective strategy to enhance supply chain resilience by improving responsiveness, minimizing performance dips during disruptions, and placing inventory buffers more intelligently.
